ECG in MRI


ECG signals are regularly acquired during a MRI examination, mainly for two reasons: (i) monitoring of the patient (ii) synchronisation of image acquisition with the heart activity. The ECG is nevertheless corrupted by different artefacts induced by (i) the magnetic field gradients or (ii) by the MagnetoHydrodynamic effect, which is induced by the blood flow in a large magnetic field.

The processing of the ECG signals is therefore required in order to ensure accurate peak detection and monitoring. I have proposed a Bayesian filtering approach for both the reduction of the magnetic field gradients and the suppression of the MagnetoHydrodynamic effect.

Signal Quality


One of the main problematic of physiological signal processing is the presence of noise, which can impact any automatic analysis and lead to false diagnostic or decision. We have decided to deal with this problem by developing a technique for the estimation of the Signal Quality Index (SQI). This technique is based on a machine learning approach, and several “basic” SQI are nonlinearly mixed in order to form an accurate estimation of the signal quality. More importantly, we have evaluated this approach several datasets containing pathological data, as such SQI should be robust to pathological data.

We have also recently demonstrated the strength of this approach by winning the Physionet/Computing in Cardiology 2014. A multimodal heart beat detector was developed, with an heuristic switching between the different available signals based on SQIs.

Arrhythmia Detection


Analysis of the ECG implies the recognition of abnormal rhythms. Such abnormal rhythms can be induced either in the atria or the ventricles, and they will have different properties.

For example, Atrial Fibrillation (AF) is the most common cardiac arrhythmia, and its prevalence is increasing with age. AF is associated with irregular and unpredictable RR intervals, due to a chaotic firing of the Atrioventricular node. We have developed a machine learning approach, mixing several features of RR unpredictability, to detect short episodes of AF on RR interval time-series.

We have also recently developed a Bayesian filtering approach for the detection of Ventricular beats. This technique is based on the model of the ECG morphology, which is estimated for both normal and ventricular beats, and a Switching Kalman filter is applied on the data for simultaneous signal denoising and beat detection. The introduction of an additional “X-Factor” mode allows for the exclusion of noisy or unknown heartbeats.

mHealth


We have developed an innovative end-to-end system allowing us to acquire ECG signal on a mobile phone. The ECG signal is then analysed on the phone. This analysis includes heartbeat detection, SQI estimation, and AF detection. The data can then be uploaded on a Medical record system, which can be accessed by clinicians for further analysis, and also where annotations from multiple clinicians can be aggregated.

Foetal ECG


I have also been involved in a foetal ECG project. We proposed accurate and innovative techniques for the extraction of foetal R peaks from maternal abdominal recordings. We have been involved in the organisation of the Physionet/Computing in Cardiology 2013, in which we have also taken part. We have been unofficial winners of the events 1 and 2.

We have also developed a realistic model of foetal ECG recordings. This code has been made available on Physionet, under the name FECGSyn.















Finally, we have also proposed new techniques for the analysis of the FECG morphology, especially QT measurement.
